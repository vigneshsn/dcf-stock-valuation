DCF Stock Valuation

Value a Stock with Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

Learn how to estimate what a stock is truly worth not just what the market says. This simple step-by-step guide helps you value a company using future cash flow projections.

Why Value a Stock?

Valuing a stock helps you make smarter investing decisions. Instead of blindly buying or selling based on the market price, you understand what the business is actually worth based on the cash it can generate in the future.

Benjamin Graham, the legendary value investor and author of The Intelligent Investor, compared the stock market to a person named “Mr. Market.” Sometimes he’s excited and offers stocks at high prices, sometimes he’s fearful and sells them cheap. By doing your own valuation, you can decide whether Mr. Market’s price is a bargain or a trap.

💡 Example: If Mr. Market offers a stock at ₹100 but your valuation says it’s worth ₹150 — that’s a potential buying opportunity!

What is Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)?

Think of a business as a machine that prints cash every year. Warren Buffett often calls this the business’s “owner earnings” — the real cash a company can generate for its owners after paying for all necessary expenses and maintenance.

The Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model asks: “How much are all those future cash prints worth in today’s money?” Because a rupee today is worth more than a rupee in the future, we discount future cash to its present value.

Add up these discounted future cash flows, include a terminal value for the years beyond your forecast, and you’ll get a reasonable estimate of what the company is worth today — its intrinsic value.

Owner Earnings

Owner earnings represent the real cash a company makes for its owners after paying all necessary expenses and maintaining its operations. Unlike accounting profits, it shows how much money is truly available for shareholders — to pay dividends, reduce debt, or reinvest for growth.

Think of this as the company’s “take-home pay” — what’s left after running the business and maintaining its assets.

Formula:
Owner Earnings = Net Income + Depreciation & Amortization − Capital Expenditures

How to Use This Calculator

Start by entering a few key financial figures — such as revenue, net profit, capital expenditure, and depreciation — which you can easily find on Screener.in or in the company’s annual report. It’s best to take the 3-year average to smooth out one-time spikes.

The calculator projects the company’s future owner earnings, discounts them to the present value, and adds the terminal value. The result is your estimate of intrinsic value.

Finally, apply the Margin of Safety principle: buy only if the current market price is 20–30% below your estimated value. This cushions you against forecast errors and market volatility.

A 20% margin of safety is applied to the fair value for a conservative estimate.
