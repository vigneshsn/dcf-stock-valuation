DCF Stock Valuation

Value a Stock with Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

A simple, step-by-step guide for stock valuation. By following this article you learn how to value a stock

Why value a stock?

Valuing a stock gives you a reasonable estimate of what the business is worth based on the cash it can generate in the future instead of blindly buying or selling based on the market price. Benjamin Graham author of book intelligent investor calls the stock market a person named "Mr. Market" sometimes he's euphoric and offers you bargains, sometimes he's panicked and overpriced. By valuing the company yourself, you decide whether Mr. Market's price is an opportunity or a warning.

Discounted Cash Flow

Think of a business as a machine that prints cash every year. Warren Buffett often calls this the business’s “owner earnings” — the real cash a company can generate for its owners after all the necessary expenses to maintain the business.

The Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model asks: “How much are all those future cash prints worth in today’s money?” Because a euro today is worth more than a euro in the future, we “discount” (shrink) future cash to today’s value. Add up the discounted cash for a number of years, include a terminal value for cash beyond your forecast horizon, and you get an estimate of the company’s total worth.

Key insight: Focus on real cash generation (owner earnings) — that’s what DCF is trying to value.

Owner earning

Owner earnings represent the real cash a company makes for its owners after paying all necessary expenses and maintaining its business operations. Unlike accounting profits, it shows how much money is truly available for investors — to pay dividends, reduce debt, or reinvest for growth

Owner Earnings = Net Income + Depreciation & Amortization − Capital Expenditures ± Changes in Working Capital
